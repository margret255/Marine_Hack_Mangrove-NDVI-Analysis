Kenya's Mangrove Ecosystem

Mangroves are trees and shrubs that grow in intertidal zones along tropical and sub-tropical coastlines. In Kenya, mangroves occupy protected shorelines, lagoons, creeks and estuaries distributed along the 600 km coastline.

Mangroves cover about 61,271 hectares (approximately 3% of Kenya's total natural forest area), with the majority in Lamu County.

Ecological Benefits

• Shoreline protection during tropical storms

• Nutrient cycling due to high productivity

• Climate change mitigation through carbon storage

• Habitat for marine wildlife

Economic Benefits

• Timber for construction and fuelwood

• Tannins for cloth dyeing and leather processing

• Nectar for beekeeping and honey production

• Wood for boats and fishing stakes

Threats to Mangroves

• Overexploitation for building poles, fencing, and charcoal

• Selective harvesting reducing biodiversity

• Pollution from oil spills and sediment contamination

• Conversion of areas for salt production

Constitution of Kenya (Article 69)

Mandates sustainable exploitation, utilization and conservation of the environment and natural resources. Requires maintaining at least 10% tree cover of Kenya's land area (including mangroves which constitute ~3%).

Forest Act (2005)

Recognizes mangrove management as protected under Article 69. Allows formation of community forest associations (like Lamu Environment Foundation) and regulates mangrove harvesting through licensing.

Forest Conservation and Management Act (2016)

Permits community associations to participate in sustainable forest management. Strengthens protection through specific harvesting regulations.

National Mangrove Ecosystem Management Plan (2017-2027)

Engages stakeholders in restoration activities with best-practice approaches at community and institutional levels. Includes ecosystem attributes, challenges, and step-by-step restoration guidance.

Lamu Beekeeping Initiative

The Lamu Environment Foundation provides grants for beekeepers, increasing apiculture while reducing mangrove degradation. Studies show bees thrive near mangroves, creating a sustainable livelihood alternative.

Learn more →

Mkupe Village Conservation

Youth Pawa organization replants mangroves to create marine habitats. Their efforts have increased fish, crab, and prawn populations, encouraging marine aquaculture over timber cutting.

Learn more →

Kilifi Nursery Program

Seatrees organization's mangrove nurseries in Marereni forest have created jobs while protecting against storm surges. The program reduces overreliance on timber products.
